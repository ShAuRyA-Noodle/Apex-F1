## 1. EDITORIAL THESIS

### What Apex Is

Apex is the **strategist's seat** in Formula 1 media. We sit between the pit wall and the grandstand — close enough to the engineering truth to call a tyre delta in seconds, close enough to the fan to feel why Hamilton's third-stint radio mattered. We write for the person who pauses the broadcast at lap 28 to check the gap chart. We do not write for the person who wants to know what Lando posted on Instagram.

Three principles govern every paragraph:

1. **Telemetry-grade specificity.** No "the car looked fast." Give the sector, the delta, the compound, the lap number. If we can't, we don't write the claim.
2. **One-deeper-than-everyone.** Every Apex piece must contain at least one observation, datapoint, or framing that no other F1 outlet ran that week. If it's already on The Race, we kill the angle and find a new one.
3. **Editorial restraint.** We don't churn rumours. We don't run "X reportedly considering Y" laundering pieces. We don't use the word "stunning."

### What Apex Isn't

Not a news aggregator. Not a meme account. Not a Drive to Survive recap show. Not "10 things you missed from Sunday." Not a paywall masquerading as journalism.

We don't compete with Formula1.com (official, captive). We don't compete with PlanetF1 (volume play, SEO factory). We compete with **The Race**, **Autosport's analysis vertical**, **The Athletic's F1 coverage (when Ronaldinho Schmitt isn't on holiday)**, and the upper 5% of Reddit posts on r/F1Technical.

### Three Writers We Study

1. **Mark Hughes (The Race / Motor Sport)** — The patron saint. Hughes' party trick is making the reader understand a mid-corner aero balance shift through one diagram-free paragraph. We steal his pacing: setup → mechanism → consequence → "and that is why Verstappen lost half a second through Maggotts." Every Apex technical piece runs the Hughes 4-beat.

2. **Joe Saward (joesaward.wordpress.com / GP+ Magazine)** — Politics, FIA backroom, F1 commercial structure. Reads paddock body language for a living. We steal his refusal to break a confidence and his habit of contextualising one weekend's controversy against a 40-year arc. Apex's "paddock corner" newsletter section is a direct Saward tribute.

3. **Karun Chandhok (Sky F1 / The Race)** — Driver-eye explanations of why a corner is hard. Apex's onboard breakdown format borrows his "I'm going to talk you through Sector 2 from inside the cockpit" energy. He turns visualisation into education without ever feeling textbook.

**Honourable mentions we also study:** Edd Straw (The Race) for tone discipline, Gary Anderson (The Race) for engineer-voice, Andrew Benson (BBC) for restraint, Adam Cooper (Motorsport.com) for paddock sourcing, **Tobi Grüner (Auto Motor und Sport)** for technical scoop velocity.

### Tone Descriptors

**Tone (3 words):** **Composed. Forensic. Cinematic.**
**Anti-tone (3 words):** **Breathless. Speculative. Listicle.**

If a draft sounds like it could appear on PlanetF1, Total Motorsport, or RacingNews365 — kill it.

## 2. CONTENT FORMATS

### F1 — Race Preview
**Definition:** Pre-weekend strategic primer. 900-1,200 words. Three-act structure: (1) track DNA + relevant history, (2) the strategic question this weekend asks, (3) drivers/teams under pressure. Always includes weather forecast, tyre allocation, and one undercovered storyline.

- *Spain GP Preview: Why Turn 10 Will Decide The Weekend*
- *Canada Preview: The Wall of Champions Is The Most Honest Corner in F1*

### F2 — Race Recap (Long)
**Definition:** Sunday-night long-form, published 22:00 local. 1,800-2,400 words. Must answer: what won the race, what story did the race tell, what does it change about the championship. Embeds 3+ telemetry/lap-time visualisations. Never a chronological lap-by-lap.

- *How McLaren's Lap 18 Undercut Was Already Lost By Lap 12*
- *Imola Was Verstappen's Most Important Win Since Brazil 2024 — Here's Why*

### F3 — Race Recap (Quick)
**Definition:** Sunday-night, 09:00 local Monday at the absolute latest. 400-600 words. Result, podium, championship implication, one chart, one quote. The piece your boss reads at breakfast.

- *Norris Wins Monaco, Verstappen Damage Control as Title Gap Falls to 11*
- *Five-Minute Spain: Antonelli's First Podium, Mercedes' Setup Gamble Pays*

### F4 — Technical Deep-Dive
**Definition:** Engineering-led explainer. 1,400-2,000 words. Built around a single mechanism: a sidepod concept, a front-wing flex test, a brake-duct trick. Must include a diagram or annotated photo and a Hughes-style mechanism paragraph. Sourced from team statements, FIA technical directives, and identifiable paddock photography — never speculation.

- *Inside Ferrari's Pull-Rod Front Suspension Rebirth: Why It's 2026's Defining Choice*
- *The McLaren Floor Edge That Made Three Teams File a Clarification Request*

### F5 — Strategy Breakdown
**Definition:** Post-race or pre-race race-strategy piece. 1,000-1,400 words. The model that says "team X had a 0.4s/lap delta on hards but the safety car at lap 31 collapsed their offset." Built off OpenF1 timing data + Pirelli compound notes. The Apex signature format.

- *The 2.3-Second Decision: How Red Bull Lost Monaco at Lap 14*
- *Why a One-Stop Was Always Faster at Spain — Mercedes Read It, McLaren Didn't*

### F6 — Driver Feature
**Definition:** Long-read profile. 2,000-3,000 words. Built on three pillars: one new framing of the driver's career, one technical/performance datapoint nobody else has surfaced, one quote from a person who actually knows them. Not a "ten things you didn't know" listicle.

- *Antonelli Was Never Really a Rookie: Inside Mercedes' Three-Year Hidden Apprenticeship*
- *The Quiet Ascent of Oscar Piastri: A Career Built on Refusing to Be Interesting*

### F7 — Team Feature
**Definition:** Org-level profile. 1,800-2,400 words. How a team is built, who decides what, what culture produces what car. Reads like a business case study with race results as the P&L.

- *Inside Audi's First Year: How Sauber Became a German Engineering Project Again*
- *What Andrea Stella Actually Changed at McLaren — A 24-Month Forensic*

### F8 — Rivalry Timeline
**Definition:** Two-driver or two-team conflict reconstructed across multiple seasons. 1,600-2,200 words. Year-by-year scrollytelling structure. Built for shareability and as the on-ramp for new fans pulled in by Drive to Survive.

- *Verstappen vs Norris: A Friendship That Was Always Going to Break*
- *Mercedes vs Red Bull, 2021-2025: The Four Acts of The Modern Era's Defining Rivalry*

### F9 — Quiz
**Definition:** Interactive 8-15 question quiz. Built on JSON schema, rendered client-side, shareable result card. Two flavours: (a) trivia ("Name the team for every Monaco GP winner since 2000"), (b) judgement ("Would you have pitted? Make the calls a strategist made.").

- *Can You Name Every Pole-Sitter From the 2024 Season?*
- *You're the Strategist: Re-Run Monaco 2026 — Would You Have Boxed Sainz on Lap 27?*

### F10 — Photo Essay
**Definition:** 8-12 image scroll-led visual narrative. Curated, captioned cinematically, attribution rigid (Wikimedia Commons + credited photographers we have licence agreements with — never random Twitter rips). 300-600 words of editorial overlay.

- *The First Lap of the Season, Frame by Frame*
- *Night Racing: Twelve Years of Singapore in Twelve Images*

### F11 — Hot Take
**Definition:** Opinion column. 600-900 words. Signed, opinionated, defensible. Strictly capped at 2/week to keep credibility. Must have a thesis sentence in the first paragraph and engage with the strongest opposing argument before closing.

- *The Constructors' Championship Is Broken — And No One Wants to Fix It*
- *Sprint Races Don't Have a Product Problem. They Have an Editorial One.*

### F12 — Mailbag / Fan Q&A
**Definition:** Weekly. 1,000-1,500 words. 5-8 reader questions answered with the same rigour as a feature paragraph. Questions sourced via newsletter reply, Reddit, and a dedicated `/ask` page. Best-in-class reference: **Tim Hauraney's mailbag at TSN**, **The Athletic NHL mailbags**.

- *Mailbag: Is Hamilton's Ferrari Year Actually Salvageable, and Other Questions*
- *Mailbag: Did Sprint Saturday Save F1 or Break It? Plus: Your Antonelli Doubts*

### F13 — Historical Retrospective (1950-2025)
**Definition:** Apex's structural moat. We have Jolpica + Wikidata feeding 75 years of cleaned data. Every retrospective is a long-form essay re-examining a moment, season, or driver from that archive. 2,000-3,500 words. Three subtypes:
- **The Forgotten Race** (e.g. 1971 Italian GP, the closest finish in F1 history)
- **The Misremembered Season** (e.g. 1982 — was it actually the most chaotic ever?)
- **The Career Re-Read** (e.g. why Gilles Villeneuve was the most overrated genius in F1)

- *The 1976 Title That Lauda Actually Did Win — A Reconstruction From The Data*
- *Jim Clark at Spa, 1963: The Most Complete Drive Anyone Has Ever Produced*

### F14 — Newsletter (Race-Week Briefing)
**Definition:** Thursday 19:00 local-market send. 800-1,200 words. Eight-block fixed format (see §5). Race weeks only get the briefing; off-weeks get a "Playlist of the Week" alt format.

- *Spain GP: McLaren's Setup Gamble + Why Antonelli's Quietly Built A Floor*
- *Off-week: The Five Best Stints of the Season So Far (with Telemetry)*

## 4. SEO TOPIC CLUSTER STRATEGY

### Architecture

Hub-and-spoke. Ten pillar pages, each ~3,000-5,000 words, evergreen, server-rendered ISR with 24h revalidation. Each pillar links down to 5 supporting articles. Each supporting article links back to the pillar via canonical breadcrumb. Cross-pillar interlinks where logically defensible only — no link farms.

### Ten Pillar Pages

| # | Pillar | Primary Keyword | Update Cadence |
|---|--------|----------------|----------------|

| P2 | **Every F1 World Champion (1950-2025)** | "f1 world champions list" (22k/mo) | Annual + new champ |
| P3 | **Lewis Hamilton Career: Complete Statistical Profile** | "lewis hamilton career stats" (18k/mo) | Quarterly |

### Five Supporting Articles Per Pillar (example: P3 — Hamilton)

1. *Hamilton's First McLaren Year, 2007: A Race-By-Race Audit*
2. *The 2008 Title Fight: What The Final Lap Of Brazil Actually Looked Like*
3. *Hamilton vs Rosberg, 2014-2016: The Inside Story*
4. *Hamilton's Best Drive: Five Candidates, Ranked*

Same structure repeats for the other nine pillars. **Total: 60 pages of evergreen content** built progressively over 6 months.

### Internal Linking Topology

- **Pillar → Spoke:** every spoke linked from pillar's TOC, named anchor.
- **Spoke → Pillar:** canonical breadcrumb + 2 contextual links inside body.
- **Spoke → Spoke (same pillar):** "Related" rail at bottom, max 3 links.
- **Cross-pillar:** only via shared entities (e.g. Hamilton spoke mentioning Monaco → P5 link). Limit 1 per article.
- **News articles → Pillars:** every news article auto-links to the entity pillar of the driver/team/circuit mentioned. Built via Drizzle entity-tagging.

### Long-Tail Keyword Strategy

Target the question-shaped queries Formula1.com refuses to answer. Use **Ahrefs / Semrush** keyword gap analysis against Formula1.com, PlanetF1, RacingNews365.

High-priority long-tail seeds (~150 to chase Year 1):

- "how many points for fastest lap 2026"
- "why was f1 2014 so dominant for mercedes"
- "what is undercut in f1"
- "longest f1 race in history"
- "f1 driver with most podiums never won championship"

Each long-tail gets a 600-1,000 word focused answer page, optimised for **Featured Snippet** + **People Also Ask**. Schema'd as `FAQPage` or `Article` depending on intent.

## 5. NEWSLETTER FORMAT — "RACE WEEK BRIEFING"

### Subject Line Patterns

Five tested patterns to A/B:

1. **{Circuit} arrives. {Specific stake}.** → *Spa arrives. McLaren can't afford another safety-car bet.*
2. **What I'd watch in {Circuit}.** → *What I'd watch in Monaco.*
3. **{Number} things about {GP} that aren't on Formula1.com.** → *Five things about Silverstone that aren't on Formula1.com.*
4. **{Driver}'s {Specific stake} weekend.** → *Antonelli's prove-it weekend.*
5. **Race Week: {Provocation}.** → *Race Week: McLaren is faster than the standings say.*

Avoid: clickbait punctuation, ALL CAPS, emoji in subject (kills inbox-provider deliverability tiers; Substack data backs this).

### Section Structure (Race-Week Edition)

Eight blocks, fixed order, fixed names. Readers learn the rhythm.

1. **THE COUNTDOWN** — 2 lines. Days to lights out, this week's session schedule in reader's local TZ.
2. **THE PREVIEW** — 180-250 words. The strategic question the weekend asks.
3. **STRATEGY INTEL** — 120-180 words. Tyre allocation, Pirelli compound choice, expected stop count, weather risk.
4. **DRIVER TO WATCH** — 100-150 words. Not the favourite — the one with the angle nobody else is writing.
5. **PADDOCK CORNER** — 100-150 words. One paddock observation. Joe Saward voice. No rumour-laundering.
6. **STANDINGS RECAP** — Table or 60-word summary. Drivers' + constructors' top 5, deltas.
7. **THREE MUST-READS** — 3 links: 1 Apex piece, 1 outside piece we admire, 1 archive piece.
8. **THE CALL TO ACTION** — Forward this. Reply with your strategy call. Join Apex+.

### Length & Send Time

- **Length:** 800-1,200 words (sweet spot per Substack 2024 deliverability data).
- **Send time:** **Thursday 19:00 in primary subscriber timezone.** Geo-segment by IP-inferred TZ. Three sends: 19:00 UK, 19:00 US-ET, 19:00 IST. Backed by Mailchimp benchmarks + The Athletic Sports newsletter ops post.

### Sample Newsletter — Spain GP 2026 (Race Week)

**Subject:** *Spain arrives. McLaren can't afford another setup gamble.*

---

**THE APEX RACE WEEK BRIEFING — SPANISH GP 2026**

---

**THE COUNTDOWN**
3 days to lights out. FP1 Friday 13:30 CET / 12:30 BST / 17:00 IST. Race Sunday 15:00 CET.

---

**THE PREVIEW**

Catalunya is the calendar's most boring honest circuit. It rewards downforce, tyre management, and the team that read the long-run pace from Friday. The McLarens were quickest on race-trim sims last weekend in Monaco — but Monaco hides setup sins. Spain doesn't. By Sunday lunchtime, the championship picture will be one of three things: cemented (McLaren confirms the 2026 quickest car), reshuffled (Red Bull finds a Catalunya-specific gain), or genuinely open (Mercedes converts Antonelli's tyre-life advantage into a podium).

The strategic question this weekend is whether McLaren's race-trim pace is real, or whether Monaco's narrow-track variance was masking a Catalunya weakness Red Bull will expose in Sector 3.

---

**STRATEGY INTEL**

Pirelli has brought C2-C3-C4. Front-left graining was the story of FP3 last year and there's nothing about the 2026 car's higher floor edge that fixes it. Expect a two-stopper as the dominant call — mediums to hards, with the second stop window opening as early as lap 20 for anyone caught behind. Weather: 26°C and a 15% chance of an isolated cell late Sunday. Worth tracking, not worth pricing in.

---

**DRIVER TO WATCH**

Not Verstappen. Watch Antonelli. Mercedes brought the floor edge update they trialled in Imola, and Catalunya is the first track where the upgrade's mechanism — a low-speed downforce gain — actually matches the circuit's character. If the W17 is going to finish the season as a 2027 development sled or as a genuine title-fight tool, we'll know by Saturday evening.

---

**PADDOCK CORNER**

Two things from the Thursday paddock. One: Toto Wolff's body language around Andrea Stella suggests the rumoured Bottas-to-Mercedes-reserve story is closer than reported. Two: Ferrari's technical re-org notice quietly named Diego Tondi as head of the front-end aero group — the first promotion since Vasseur's reshuffle that actually changes a decision-making axis. Watch for the front-wing iteration in Austria.

---

**STANDINGS RECAP**

Drivers (top 5):
1. Norris 168
2. Verstappen 157 (-11)

## 6. AI-ASSISTED WORKFLOW

### Claude First-Draft Prompt Template

**System prompt:**

```
You are a senior F1 staff writer at Apex, an independent Formula 1 editorial platform.
Editorial principles:
1. Telemetry-grade specificity — no "the car looked fast." Always name the sector,
   lap, compound, delta, driver, team.
2. Three writers we emulate: Mark Hughes (technical pacing), Joe Saward (paddock
   restraint), Karun Chandhok (driver-eye explanation).
3. Banned phrases: stunning, sensational, mind-blowing, jaw-dropping, must-see,
   you won't believe, here's why, here's how (as headline opener).
4. Banned formats: numbered listicles, "10 things you missed" recaps,
   rumour-laundering ("X is reportedly considering Y").
5. Every claim of fact must be sourceable. If you don't know a stat, write
   [VERIFY: stat needed] inline — never invent.
6. Tone: composed, forensic, cinematic. Not breathless, speculative, listicle.
7. Output structure: lead paragraph that states the thesis in 2-3 sentences,
   then 4-6 body sections of 2-4 paragraphs each, then a 1-paragraph close
   that lands the thesis.
8. Length: match the brief target ±10%.
```

**User prompt (filled per article):**

```

  - {fact 1 + source}

  - "{quote}" — {speaker, source, date}
```

Draft is the **scaffold**, not the article. Always rewritten by human.

### Fact-Check Protocol

**The Two-Source Rule** (non-negotiable, in every article):

1. **Source 1 — Primary structured:** Jolpica F1 API (historical), OpenF1 API (current season timing), or official team press release.
2. **Source 2 — Primary unstructured:** A second non-AI source. Wikipedia is allowed as a tertiary cross-check but **never** as one of the two primary sources. Acceptable second sources: FIA press conference transcripts, team principal interview, identified paddock photograph, official broadcast, ATLAS F1 Bulletin Board archives (for historical claims).
3. **Source 3 (for sensitive claims — financial, legal, technical regulation):** A named human source or a court/FIA document.

Fact-checker (initially the editor; later a dedicated freelance fact-checker at £25/article) annotates each claim with source URLs in a `/facts/{article-slug}.md` file kept in repo. Public-facing footnotes only on technical and historical pieces.

### Human Editor Pass

Four-pass system before publish:

1. **Pass 1 — Structure & thesis** (editor, 20 min). Does the lead state the thesis? Does the close land it? Are sections doing different work?
2. **Pass 2 — Voice & tone** (editor, 15 min). Read aloud test. Any banned phrases? Any breathlessness? Any unsourced superlatives?
3. **Pass 3 — Fact-check** (fact-checker, 30 min). Every numeric claim, every name, every date verified against the two-source standard.
4. **Pass 4 — Legal & IP** (editor, 5 min). No F1/FIA marks misused. No team logos without clear editorial-use justification. No copyrighted images without licence. Disclaimer present.

### Headline A/B Testing

- **Mechanism:** Use newsletter open-rate as the signal. Send each newsletter with two subject lines split 50/50 across the list (Beehiiv and ConvertKit both support this natively at $0 tier; Substack does not — if we choose Substack, we manage A/B manually with two segments).
- **Decision threshold:** 95% confidence interval after 24h. The winner becomes the on-site headline used for the article when it crossposts to social.
- **Metric:** Open rate primarily, click-through-to-article secondarily.
- **Cadence:** Every newsletter. Logged in a Notion sheet so we learn patterns (questions vs declaratives, named drivers vs themes, specific numbers vs abstractions).

### Image Source SOP

**Priority order:**

1. **Wikimedia Commons** — Always check first. Attribute per file licence (CC-BY-SA 4.0 etc.) in caption: `Photo: Lukas Raich / Wikimedia Commons / CC-BY-SA 4.0`.
2. **F1 / FIA Media** — Only if we have explicit written editorial-use permission. Don't assume; ask.
3. **Independent photographers (Kym Illman, Bryn Lennon, Andy Hone via Getty)** — Licence per use through Getty Images / agencies. Budget: £35-£75 per editorial-use image. Pillar pages and headline features only.
4. **Reader-submitted (clearly licensed)** — With written release, attribution, no commercial reuse without further consent.
5. **AI-generated cinematic image** — **Last resort, always disclosed.** Caption: *Image generated by Apex using AI for editorial illustration. Not a photograph of the event.* Never used for actual race moments. Never used to depict identifiable drivers in untrue scenarios. Only for abstract / atmospheric headers when no licensable photograph exists.

**Banned:** Twitter rips, Reddit rips, screenshots from F1 TV, any image without traceable licence.

## 7. VIRAL CONTENT PATTERNS — 10 PATTERNS APEX EXPLOITS

These are social-first formats, not website articles. Each one feeds traffic upstream to a longer Apex piece. We don't chase virality; we engineer 10-20% of monthly social impressions to come from one of these.

1. **"Did you know" race-fact micros** — Tweet + Instagram carousel. One verified historical fact (Jolpica-sourced). Format: hook line, fact, "context paragraph," link to relevant Apex archive piece. *e.g. "Did you know — the 1971 Italian GP had the closest finish in F1 history. 0.01 seconds across the line. Five cars within 0.61s. We unpacked it →"*

2. **Onboard lap analysis with sector overlay** — TikTok / YouTube Short / Twitter video. 45-second clip with sector timing overlay, two-line voiceover, freeze-frame on the apex of the corner that won the lap. Format pioneered by **Driver61 (Scott Mansell)** — we do it tighter and weekly.

3. **"If [driver] had [decision]" counterfactuals** — Twitter thread. 6-8 tweets. Re-run a race with one strategic decision changed, using actual delta-per-lap data. The best version of this format on Twitter is by **F1Stats / F1DataAnalysis** — we do it editorially polished.

4. **Wholesome paddock moments compilations** — Instagram Reel / TikTok. 30 seconds, six clips, no voiceover, ambient sound. Drive to Survive audience eats this. Low effort, high reach.

5. **DTS-driven character arc threads** — Twitter / Threads. For the new-fan audience. "If you only watched Drive to Survive S6, here's what actually happened in 2024." Bridges the casual-to-engaged gap.

6. **Pre-race tyre-strategy predictions ("post yours")** — Tweet pinned Thursday. Apex's prediction, then "what's your call?" Drives reply-engagement, which drives algorithm. Settle on Monday with the result thread.

7. **Quiz format mid-race ("guess who's leading after lap 15")** — Twitter poll. Live during race. Format borrowed from cricket Twitter (Cricbuzz does this brilliantly). 4-option poll, reveal at the actual lap.

8. **Historic-vs-modern comparison ("Schumacher 2000 vs Verstappen 2025 at the same corner")** — TikTok / Reel / YouTube Short. Side-by-side onboard from the Apex archive. The lowest-effort archive-monetisation format we have. Patrick Boyle of **Chain Bear** has perfected this for YouTube.

9. **Polls — "would you rather"** — Twitter / Instagram Stories. Light, daily. Builds the "second screen during boring midweek" habit. *"Would you rather: one championship like Hamilton 2008, or four like Verstappen 2021-24?"*

10. **Behind-the-scenes editorial process posts** — Twitter / LinkedIn. *Here's the spreadsheet we used to build the Monaco recap. Here's the source list. Here's the angle we killed.* Twitter and LinkedIn audiences (especially journalism Twitter) reward transparency. This is also our recruiting tool.

## 9. REDDIT / SOCIAL-MEDIA GROWTH PLAYBOOK

### r/formula1 (1.6M+ subs) — Org-Account Posting Rules

The single rule that makes or breaks Apex on Reddit: **r/formula1 hates self-promo and bans it on sight.** Path to legitimacy:

1. **Apply for verified press flair** via the mod team. Requires (a) consistent on-site editorial output (3+ months of weekly publication minimum), (b) demonstrable original reporting, (c) named editorial masthead. Modelled on how **The Race, RaceFans, Auto Motor und Sport** got flair.
2. **Until flaired: do not post.** Period. Comment helpfully on others' threads with the Apex account, build comment karma, build mod relationships. The Reddit comms team of **The Athletic** spent 4 months in comments before posting a single article.
3. **Once flaired:** post only original analysis pieces, not aggregation. Cap at 2 posts per week. Always engage with replies for the first 4 hours.
4. **Title format that survives:** factual, no clickbait, includes the substantive claim. *"Apex analysis: McLaren's lap 18 undercut at Spain was already lost by lap 12 — telemetry breakdown"* survives. *"You won't BELIEVE what we found in the Spain GP data"* gets removed in 3 minutes.

### Subreddit-by-Subreddit Tactics

- **r/F1Technical (~150k)** — The most rewarding subreddit for Apex's voice. Post Technical Deep-Dives and Strategy Breakdowns here. Engagement is low-volume but high-quality. Karun-voice content scores. The mod team is welcoming to credentialed sites that bring real engineering content.
- **r/F1Memes (~330k)** — Don't post. Pattern-match the meme cycle to inform our social team about what casual fans cared about that weekend. It's an intelligence source, not a distribution channel.
- **r/F1FanArt (~85k)** — Sponsor a monthly fan-art prize (£100, judged by Apex editorial). Builds goodwill cheaply.
- **r/formuladank (~440k)** — Lurk only. Tonal intelligence for what's being mocked.
- **r/RaceTrackDesigns, r/F1Game** — Niche but high-affinity. Cross-post relevant technical / historical pieces. Lower volume, higher loyalty.

### Twitter/X Account Structure

- **@apex_f1** (main) — Every article posted. Race-week threads. Polls. Editorial voice. Target: 50k followers in 12 months. Realistic Year 1 ceiling without paid amplification: 25-30k.
- **@apex_strategy** (secondary, launch Month 4) — Strategy-only account. Pre-race tyre calls, live race strategy threads, post-race breakdowns. Builds the "smart F1 Twitter" reputation. Reference: **@F1DataAnalysis, @TheBlackBookF1**.
- **@apex_history** (secondary, launch Month 6) — Archive-only. Daily "On This Day in F1" + weekly long-form thread from a historical retrospective. The lowest-effort high-engagement account once the 1950-2025 archive is queryable. Reference: **@SeanKellyF1, @F1Stat**.

All three accounts cross-promote sparingly. Each account has a distinct voice — main is editorial, strategy is engineering, history is archival.

### TikTok Content Adaptation

- **Cadence:** 4-6 short-form videos per race weekend; 2-3 per off-week.
- **Format priority:** (1) onboard with sector overlay, (2) historic-vs-modern comparison, (3) wholesome paddock compilation, (4) "you are the strategist" interactive.
- **Production:** £200-£400/race-weekend for a freelance video editor through Month 6, then hire FT video producer Month 12 if metrics warrant.
- **Goal:** 50k followers by Month 12. Tertiary funnel — primarily a discovery surface that drives newsletter signups via bio link.

### YouTube Channel Decision

**Recommendation: partner first, build own Month 12.**

Reasons:
- YouTube production cost is non-trivial (~£600-£1,000 per polished long-form video including editor, motion designer, voiceover talent if not the founder).
- Algorithm rewards consistency at the >1/week cadence — that's a £30k/year commitment minimum.
- Better Year 1 path: **co-produce / contribute analysis to existing established F1 YouTubers** with audience (Aldas, Chain Bear, Tommo F1, F1Elvis). Provide them Apex data + analysis credits. Earn 50-200k impressions per video at near-zero cost.
- Month 12: if newsletter is past 25k subs AND social is past 100k followers AND we've raised a seed round, hire a YouTube producer and launch the channel.

Pay top-of-band for the first 3 hires. The Year 1 narrative of Apex internally and externally is that we pay properly and edit thoughtfully — that earns us the third hire on inbound rather than outbound. Compensation undercut is the fastest way to lose to PlanetF1's recruiting machine.
